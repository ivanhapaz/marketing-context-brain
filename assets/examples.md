# Worked excerpts

**Read these for shape, not for content.** Every fact below is invented. Acme's answers are not your client's answers — if any Acme specifics show up in a real brain you build, you've used this file wrong.

These are the three documents where prose can't convey what "good" means. The other eight are structurally obvious once you've seen these.

Fictional company: **Acme** — a SaaS tool that turns customer support tickets into product bug reports. Sells to product and engineering teams at 50–500 person software companies.

---

## Voice & Tone — excerpt

The point of a principle is that it's a *check*. If the agent can't use it to reject its own draft, it isn't a principle, it's a mood.

### P3. Name the mechanism, not the outcome

Support teams have read "save hours every week" a thousand times. They believe verbs they can picture.

✅ *Acme reads the ticket thread, pulls the console errors, and writes the repro steps.*
❌ *Acme saves your team hours of manual triage.*

The ❌ line isn't false. It's just unfalsifiable, which reads as marketing. The ✅ line makes a claim a skeptical engineer can check against their own workflow.

### P4. Never make the reader the problem

Our buyer inherited their process. They didn't design it, and they already know it's bad.

✅ *Ticket triage is somebody's Monday. It doesn't have to be.*
❌ *Stop wasting your engineers' time on manual triage.*

The ❌ line implies the reader chose this. Nobody forwards an ad that scolds them.

### Lexicon

| Use | Never | Why |
|---|---|---|
| ticket, thread, repro | issue, item, incident | Their words, not ITSM vendor words |
| triage | ticket management | What practitioners actually call it |
| engineers, support | resources, headcount | We're not selling to a spreadsheet |
| — | seamless, effortless, game-changing | Category noise; every competitor claims it |

### Variant kit — five angles on one offer

Ten variants are only ten variants if they'd survive being placed next to each other. Same offer (free trial), five genuinely different tensions:

1. **The Monday** — the specific dread of the triage queue after a weekend.
2. **The lost repro** — the bug that got closed because nobody could reproduce it.
3. **The engineer's time** — what the eng team gave up to answer a question support could have.
4. **The proof** — one customer's before/after, with the number.
5. **The demo** — the product doing the thing, no narrative at all.

An angle is a different *tension*, not a different adjective. If two variants could swap headlines without anyone noticing, they're one variant.

## ICP — excerpt

### ✅ A segment that changes the marketing

**S2 · Support-led, no dedicated triage**
*50–200 person SaaS. Support owns first response; there's no triage layer. Engineering gets pinged in Slack.*

- **Trigger:** support headcount grows faster than eng, and the Slack pings become a running joke.
- **Job to be done:** stop being the team that interrupts engineering.
- **Buys because:** it protects the support team's credibility, not because it saves money.
- **Objection:** "our tickets are too messy for a tool to parse."
- **Counter:** the messy ticket *is* the input — show a real one, unedited, and the report it produced.
- **Proof that lands:** a support lead saying eng stopped dreading their Slack messages.
- **Where they are:** support communities, not eng communities. Different rooms, different language.

Notice what makes it usable: the trigger tells you *when* to reach them, the objection tells you what the ad has to defuse, and the proof tells you which case study to pull. A different segment would need a different ad — that's the test.

### ❌ A decorative persona

**"Technical Tara, Support Manager, 32"**
*Tara is busy and values efficiency. She's tech-savvy and wants tools that just work. Her pain point is manual processes. She reads industry blogs and is active on LinkedIn.*

This fails because nothing in it changes a single marketing decision. Every competitor's Tara is identical. Swap the product name and it still fits. Age and a first name are not segmentation.

**The test:** if two segments would get the same ad, they're one segment. If a segment implies no specific ad at all, it's decoration.

## Decision Log — excerpt

Four fields per entry. The last one is the one everybody skips and the only one that lets a rule expire.

### DL-04 · Never claim a percentage of tickets auto-resolved

**The rule:** no auto-resolution stat in any ad, page, or deck.

**What happened:** an early campaign ran "resolves 40% of tickets automatically." The number came from one design-partner account with an unusually clean ticket format. Two prospects quoted it back during demos and neither saw anything close. One churned in month two and said the demo felt like a different product.

**What we rejected:** "up to 40%" (same problem, worse faith), footnoting the methodology (nobody reads the footnote, everyone remembers the number), and re-running the analysis across all accounts to find an honest average — rejected for now because the sample is still too small to be stable.

**What would reverse it:** 30+ accounts with 90 days of data and a median we'd defend in a demo. Then we quote the median, name the sample, and keep the range visible.

---

### DL-07 · Support communities only; no eng community posting

**The rule:** we don't post product content in engineering communities.

**What happened:** a well-meaning post in an eng subreddit got read as vendor spam and drew a pile-on. The thread still ranks for our name.

**What we rejected:** posting under a personal account (worse — it's the same act with a disguise), and paying a community mod for placement (undisclosed payment is the fastest way to lose a room permanently).

**What would reverse it:** an eng-side champion who wants to write about their own workflow, in their own voice, with us disclosed as their vendor and not their editor.
